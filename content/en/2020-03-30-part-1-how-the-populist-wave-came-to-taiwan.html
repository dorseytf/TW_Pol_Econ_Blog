The Global Trend Toward Populism

In recent years, we have seen authoritarian-leaning populist leaders come to power1 in countries across the world leading to a sudden renewed interest in the topic (see here,2 here,3 here,4 and here5 for just a few). But what factors are fueling this rise in support for candidates and parties that are often openly hostile6 to democracy? While exact definitions differ, among scholars of populism one of the most widely accepted7 defining characteristics8 is splitting society into “the people” and “the elites,” who are not to be trusted. Populists have seen greater success in creating this schism in recent years because their claims of grievances have gained credibility over the past several decades. This credibility comes from several factors, but two of the strongest, which we will explore here, are

  1. Unequal economic performance, and
  2. Changes in the Media landscape

How Economic Factors Lay the Groundwork for Populism

I. Inequality

For the past several decades, much of the world has seen an increase in both wealth and income inequality.9 And while there have been tremendous gains in the global economy since the 2008 recession, the vast majority of the recovery has not been shared evenly at either the national or sub-national level. Inequality has not only grown significantly10 since the crash in 2008, it is now also receiving increased attention from both economists (see Piketty’s meteoric rise to fame) and the population at large. Even in countries where numerous aggregate economic indicators suggest that the economy has recovered to pre-crisis levels, such as the USA, this growing inequality is causing discontent that is being successfully leveraged by populist candidates like Donald Trump.

Figure 1: Wealth Share by Wealth Decile in the USA (from Bruenig in Jacobin, 2017)

Figure 2: Income Inequality has Risen in Most OECD Countries

II. Stagnant Earnings and Unemployment

In addition to rising inequality, many countries have struggled with persistent high unemployment for large swaths of their population, and particularly younger workers in countries like Greece,11 Spain,12 and Italy.13 Even for those fortunate enough to have employment, earnings everywhere are underperforming relative to past trends. Economists have found robust evidence that workers’ wages are no longer growing14 alongside productivity15 in many countries. In addition, younger workers also earn significantly less16 than their parents’ generation.17

Figure 3: Productivity and Wages have Decoupled in the US

III. Housing Prices

In conjunction with the downward pressures on income and wealth felt by most people, housing prices have continued to rise quickly.18 This has led to a severe drop in home purchases among the young, overcrowding, and an ever-increasing share of income being devoted toward housing,19 particularly among low-income families.

Figure 4: OECD Housing Indicators

Another useful metric for examining housing affordability relative to incomes is the housing median multiple measure (see Demographia’s Middle-Income Housing Affordability20 report), which takes the median home price divided by the median household income to measure relative affordability. Values of this metric are categorized as such:

Combine poor job prospects and unaffordable housing with the increased concentration of wealth at the top and populists’ appeals to the masses against a hated elite suddenly seem far more credible. Successful populists then leverage this credibility to garner support for their own preferred solutions, reframing the policy debate to their advantage and often catching established politicians off guard.

How Populists Use a Changing Media Landscape

Because populists rely on media coverage to distance themselves from establishment politicians and gain new supporters by claiming to reveal truths and raise issues the “elites” refuse to discuss, the media plays a critical role in the growth of populist movements. There are two major media trends that have actually aided the current populist wave in recent decades. First, the growth of partisan media outlets, in both traditional and social media, has reframed much of the mainstream discourse and allowed some people to self-select21 more attitude-consistent content. Secondly, populists have been able to reach greater audiences22 and get constant coverage to reinforce and legitimize their talking points (see Mazzoleni in Twenty-First Century Populism23) due to the complicity of traditional media24 outlets and the ubiquity of social media.

I. Partisanship in the Media

Since the rise of clearly partisan news networks25 and online media outlets, many researchers have feared, and found at least some evidence26 for, “echo chambers” or “filter bubbles”, where people choose to get more information from sources that align with their beliefs. While experts are still hotly debating the extent27 and impact of28 “echo chambers”29 for the population at large, there is growing evidence that at least the more politically active, highly partisan sections of the population are engaging in selective exposure.

While a great deal of research suggests that because its viewership is much smaller than mainstream media outlets, the impact of this growing partisan media is limited (and often appears to have a small effect in regression analysis), there is strong evidence that the overall impact of partisan media can be difficult to measure. Research shows that the viewpoints expressed in partisan media spread beyond just its viewership, and can actually be spread quite effectively via interpersonal communication.30 The impact of this two-step communication flow can now be magnified by populists and their supporters with the help of social media, where those who self-select more partisan news sources can spread their viewpoints to more people more often. This is one area that needs more empirical research, though, as previous studies have mostly looked at rather people got their news from social media via news outlets and not from friends or media personalities.

Additionally, while research shows that a large majority of people still get their news from a variety of mainstream sources (and many researchers argue that fears of echo chambers are overblown), content analysis31 of opinion articles across Europe over the same period show that public debates have become more populist over time. Even in mainstream media outlets, topics have shifted to have more overlap with populist talking points, which has likely contributed to legitimizing32 these talking points. This ideological shift appears to correlate strongly with the electoral success of populist parties in Europe.

II. Greater Audiences

As mentioned above, mainstream media outlets are also often complicit in giving populists the attention33 they crave. Even with largely negative coverage, media are providing populists a platform that they can use to reach more people, and often this negative coverage plays right into the populists’ claims that the “establishment” does not want them to be heard. Esser, Stepinska, and Hopmann (in Populist Political Communication in Europe, 2016) point out several cases34 where European populists parties experienced electoral success following largely negative mainstream media coverage.

Even when the mainstream media does not adopt or discuss their talking points, social media provides another avenue for populists to amplify their visibility.35 While politicians and parties across the spectrum utilize social media, it is particularly well-suited to populists because of its largely unregulated, non-hierarchical, and democratic structure (see Bartlett36 in European Populism and Winning the Immigration Debate, 2014). Since social media provides information separately from traditional, “elite-owned” media outlets, supporters of populists tend to trust the news and information they receive via social media more, whether or not it is actually factual.

Indeed, the unfiltered nature of social media allows populists and their supporters to make claims without evidence, alter news stories to more readily align with their beliefs, and even simply fabricate false narratives. In response to this, fact checking has grown significantly, although there is abundant37 evidence38 that it is often ineffective39 and misinformation can outnumber attempts to correct it by 3:1.40 Even when people are presented with a detailed debunking of misinformation, it often has the opposite effect, and serves to solidify their beliefs.

What Do These Factors Look Like in Taiwan?

Economic Trends in Taiwan

I. Inequality

Taiwan has not been immune to the trend of high inequality seen elsewhere, in terms of both income and wealth.

Lien, et al (2019) utilized the Individual Wealth Register from Taiwan’s Ministry of Finance to build a dataset of wealth distribution for Taiwan covering the period 2004 to 2014. Using this data, they found that, although certain assets such as stocks have concentrated toward the top, Taiwan’s Gini coefficient for overall wealth remained fairly steady around 0.79 for the period, which is comparable to South Korea and Sweden, and lower than that of the US.

Figure 6: Wealth Distribution and Average Net Worth in Taiwan

While wealth inequality in Taiwan may not have increased significantly over the period 2004-2014, it is still true that the top 1% and 10% own 23% and 61% of all wealth, respectively. This inequality is also not spread evenly across age groups: the Gini coefficient for those under 50 increased over the period, while it decreased among those over 70.

In terms of income inequality, regardless of the measure used (either Gini Coefficient or the Ratio of Income Share of the Top 20% to the bottom 20%), we see that income inequality has been steadily increasing in Taiwan since the 1980s. While Taiwan’s inequality is considerably better than in many other developed countries, the trend still has troubling implications.

Figure 7: Measures of Income Inequality in Taiwan

Looking at average savings per household, we again see a significant divergence between the top income quintile and the rest of the population.

Figure 8: Average Savings per Household by Income Quintile in Taiwan

II. Stagnant Earnings and Unemployment

While casual observers may only be familiar with Taiwan’s development success story as one of the four “Asian Tigers” (along with South Korea, Hong Kong, and Singapore) who saw dramatic growth and development beginning in the 1960s, for scholars and ordinary Taiwanese people, stagnant wages have been the biggest economic story for almost two decades.

Figure 9: Real Monthly Earnings in Taiwan have stagnated

This is largely the result of an issue that is shared with most other developed countries: productivity and wages have decoupled. This means that despite the fact that Taiwan’s GDP and worker productivity continue to grow, real wages are stuck near 2002 levels. If wages were paid in line with productivity, earnings would be significantly higher for the average worker.

Figure 10: Wages and Productivity (here measured in GDP growth) have decoupled in Taiwan as well

Even though many indicators have shown significant growth and improvement in the economy, including having the highest GDP growth rate among the Asian Tigers and the greatest increase in exports to the US, most of these improvements have not been felt evenly. In contrast to countries like South Korea and Japan, where large conglomerates account for the bulk of economic output, small- and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) account for 97% of all Taiwanese companies. In spite of this, the share of exports that come from SMEs in Taiwan’s export-dominated economy has consistently shrunk since the 90s, accounting for only 13.7% in 2018.

Figure 11: SMEs Falling Share of Taiwan’s Exports

In itself, this shift does not necessarily represent an issue. The problem arises because the firms that have grown to represent the bulk of Taiwan’s exports (mostly concentrated in the electrical equipment and parts industry) are focused on intermediate goods, and are therefore severely limited in their ability to move up the value-added chain. With trade accounting for such a large portion of Taiwan’s economy, a large number of workers and firms are essentially stuck at a lower level of value, and many observers think that this may be one of the biggest causes of the decoupling of wages and productivity.

In addition to these stagnant wages, Taiwan also has the 4th longest working hours in the world, and is second only to Singapore in Asia. Recent surveys from the Ministry of Labor have shown that 46% of Taiwanese worked at least some overtime in the last year, with an average of 15.9 hours of overtime per month. However it’s not all doom and gloom on this front. Total working hours in Taiwan are on the decline, with annual working hours decreasing by 122 since 2008. Surveys also show that about 70% of employees are largely satisfied with their jobs.

III. Housing Prices

As in many other countries around the world, housing prices have also risen significantly in Taiwan relative to incomes. Utilizing the same housing median multiple metric from before for Taiwan over the period 2002-2019, we see that the country as a whole has been Extremely Unaffordable for nearly the entire time. The capital Taipei fares even worse, with a housing median multiple above 10 since early 2009. Given that Taiwan’s largely mountainous terrain limits the available area for new housing, this is not entirely surprising.

Figure 12: Quarterly Housing Median Multiple for Taiwan and Taipei

We see the same picture using alternative measures, such as the Price-to-Income Ratio and the Loan-Payment-to-Income Ratio.

Figure 13: Measures of Taiwan’s Housing Affordability Crisis

While this unaffordability is offset somewhat by Taiwan’s historically low interest rates, the unaffordability crisis has hit younger workers particularly hard. Surveys show that nearly half of adults in the 25-40 age range live with their parents, and less than 30% own their own home. Around 44% of respondents in this age range also expressed interest in applying for social housing (rentals that are subsidized by the government) in order to have a chance to save enough to eventually purchase their own home.

How exactly did the housing market in Taiwan get so out of whack? In his excellent and in-depth analysis, Sheng-cheng Hu shows that it is due in large part to excess savings outpacing gross domestic investment, which led to capital outflows and heavy speculation in the stock and housing markets. In Taiwan, the household savings rate actually fell from 24.42% in 2003 to 20.62% in 2012, so the glut in excess savings was created by two forces: businesses hoarding cash (rather than reinvesting it) and Taiwanese businesses in China moving capital back into Taiwan.

In 2009, changes to tax laws meant that certain marginal tax rates fell from 50% to 10%, leading to an influx of capital from Taiwanese businesses that felt China’s coastal cities had become too expensive to be easily profitable. However, this capital influx was not accompanied by any actual plans for reinvesting in Taiwan, and in order to avoid losses in the stock market due to the Great Financial Crisis, much of it wound up in the housing market. This not only caused housing prices to increase, but it also prevented wages from keeping up with inflation as this money could have been put to better use.

All of this speculation in the housing market has left Taiwan not only with ever-increasing home prices, but with a growing number of empty houses. At the end of 2019, the number of empty houses in Taiwan was almost 860,000, and this problem was not restricted to just the capital. Despite this oversupply, the number of new houses (defined by the Ministry of the Interor as those built within the last 5 years listed as available to purchase for the first time) in the 4th Quarter of 2018 surpassed 75,000.

The Taiwanese Media Landscape

I. Democratization Brought Changes to Media

When the pro-Democracy movement finally succeeded in having Martial Law lifted in 1987 (nearly 40 years after it began), Taiwan began a series of dramatic changes across many areas. In 2003, the first non-KMT President, Chen Shui-bian, implemented a policy of releasing news and media from governmental and party control, which increased the number of legal newspapers published in Taiwan from just 31 in 1988 to 2,210 by 2011, and by 2006 cable TV had a penetration rate of almost 82% of households. Despite the large growth in the number of media outlets, traditional media, such as newspapers and TV, remained dominated by just a handful of influential oulets.

II. Chinese Influence in Traditional Media

For an in-depth analysis of China’s program of exerting influence over Taiwan’s media, I highly recommend Hsu Chien-jung’s excellent article “China’s Influence on Taiwan’s Media” in Asian Survey (2014). In short, he shows that, beginning in the late 2000’s, China began a concerted effort of encouraging pro-China tycoons to purchase media outlets and shift toward pro-China coverage, pressuring media proprietors who have invested in China, and using embedded advertising. More recently, a defected spy claimed that China has gone so far as to provide funding to several of Taiwan’s largest news companies, triads, university presidents, and even the populist presidential candidate.41

Leading up to the presidential election, many outlets, and particularly those with pro-China bias, had nonstop coverage of the populist candidate, Han Kuo-yu. Even among regular news outlets, coverage of the populist candidate was almost non-stop, and no matter what revelations were reported, from organized crime connections to misogyny to racism, nothing seemed to dent his popularity.

III. Misinformation on Social Media

As in the rest of the world, the spread of misleading information and false rumors on social media are a growing problem in Taiwan, especially in the run up to elections. These problems are exacerbated by the focused efforts of China’s misinformation campaign.

As social media and messaging apps become ubiquitious, fake news and rumors become harder to combat, with more avenues for dissemination and replication. In the run up to the 2019 presidential election in Taiwan, Facebook set up a “war room” in Taipei specifically to combat the spread of fake news before the election, and on one Friday alone closed 118 pages, 99 groups, and 51 accounts for violations of their terms of service (one of the groups, which supported the populist KMT candidate, had over 150,000 members).

Taiwan’s most popular messaging app by far is Line, with about 75% of the population already using the app by 2013 (only 2 years after its release). While it soon became one of the most common ways to communicate with friends and family, false rumors and fake news stories quickly ran rampant just as with Facebook. In Taiwan, these are often designed specifically to trick older users, who use social media widely, but are less likely to fact check claims they see. Many also argue that the group chat feature of the app can also lead to media bubbles that reinforce existing political and social divisions.

In addition to false stories that are traced back to China’s servers, another well known source of fake news in Taiwan (and the bane of anyone foolish enough to venture into the comment section of a political article on Taiwan) is China’s “50 Cent Army” of paid online trolls and commentators. These trolls are reportedly paid a small fee for each comment (hence the name), and inundate political discourse online.

The vast majority of the misinformation deluge since 2018 has been focused on either attacking the current president, Tsai Ying-wen, or supporting the China-friendly Han Kuo-yu. The populist Han experienced a meteoric rise from obscurity and unemployment to unseating the heavily-favored incumbent mayor of Kaohsiung largely due to his social media presence. However, there is ample evidence that his social media presence grew due to the work of Chinese cyber-operatives, which further propelled Han into the front page news and gave him enough credibility to gain real supporters within Taiwan.
